The Internet
Behind the Web
What Started It All
The Atomic Bomb
The threat of atomic warfare threatened to cripple a central communication network with one bomb.
Circuit Switching
The use of circuit switching required a central location that would connect multiple points of communication. This flaw lead to the idea of packet switching, and the seed of the world wide web was born.
February 7, 1958
President Eisenhower Founds ARPA
The Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA), known as DARPA today, was founded after the Soviet Union launched Sputnik 1 in 1957. One of their projects was ARPANET, and it aimed at creating a wide area packet switched network with distributed control, the foundation that the modern internet sits upon.
J.C. Licklider
The Math Behind The Idea of Modern Packet Switching
A psychologist and computer scientist by trade, it's Licklider who created the framework for the mathematics behind the idea of sending a self certifiable message over a packet switched network. Other members of ARPA and the scientific community, using his initial mathematics, helped get ARPANET off the ground.
Leonard Kleinrock
Que-ing Theory
Kleinrock's doctoral thesis at MIT was on the mathematics of Que-ing theory, which was crucial to packet switching and formed a basis for how the decentralized "nodes" will communicate over the early ARPANET.

June 1, 1969 And Beyond
Bolt Beranek and Newman (BBN)
Won the contract to Build the first IMPs
BBN Team Lead Frank Heart and team won the contract to build the ARPANET mainframe IMPs on July 1, 1969. The first IMP was delivered to UCLA and graduate student Stephen Crocker wrote the code for UCLA's computer to communicate with. Later, Bob Metcalf codes MIT's mainframe to communicate with it's IMP.
October 29, 1969 the first message over ARPANET is sent and received.
Raymond Tomlinson
Inventor of E-Mail: The @ Sign Guy!
In the span of only one year of ARPANET being online, computers are connected, files are sent, and e-mail is invented.
April 1971
18 mainframe computers are connected to ARPANET.
1972
Fewer than 25 computers on ARPANET
The very first International Conference on Computers and Communications is held in October of 1972 in Washington, DC. ARPANET is a big hit and it's future is brought that much closer to the modern internet.
1992
TCP/IP
Vince Cerf and Bob Kahn invent TCP/IP, which later in 1983 wins out against other internet protocols, and is finally named the official Internet Protocol.
ARPANET is Set Free
December 1991
Congressional Legislature expands ARPANET to the public.
Al Gore introduces a bill that injects $600 million dollars into further expansion of the ARPANET and promotes the innovation that would become the foundational technology advancements leading to the commercial Internet as we know it today.
Tim Berners-Lee
Invented the World Wide Web
Tim Berners-Lee and his team are invented HTTP and HTML, Hypetext Transfer Protocol and HyperText Markup Language. Both introduce ways for personal computers to view and display internet web pages.
1994
Birth of the Web Browser
Tim Berners-Lee founds the World Wide Web Project and later the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) with the aim at unifying standards across the web.
Marc Anderson codes the first web browser called MOSAIC, later known as Netscape. The next chapter of the Internet begins, the Browser Wars.
